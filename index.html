Mocking Asylum Interview Questions in USA

1. Interview Structure Overview & Oath

  • Do you wish to proceed in English and waive your right to an interpreter? Yes, in English.
  • Do you understand this oath form? Yes.
  • Do you promise to tell the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth? Yes, I do.

2. Review of Application & Supporting Documents

  • Did anyone help you prepare your asylum application? No, I did it by myself.
  • Is everything in your application true and correct to the best of your knowledge? Yes.
  • Are there any changes to make? No.
  • Did anyone help you prepare your personal statement or your supporting documents? No, I did that by myself too.
  • How did you obtain those support letters? I emailed them and asked. Two mailed the letters, one emailed because she was too scared to mail it.
  • Are all supporting documents true and correct? Yes.
  • Any medical conditions affecting memory? No.
  • Any medications affecting memory? No.
  • Have you presented any fraudulent documents? No.
  • Have you lied to the US government or officials? Yes. On my US visa application, I lied and said I was not arrested/detained.
  • Why did you lie? I was afraid that listing my arrest would mean no visa and being stuck. They had threatened me, and I was trying to escape.
  • Did you lie about anything else on your visa application? No, everything else was true.
  • Was everything in the visa interview true? Yes.

3. Personal Story: Basis for Asylum

  • Why are you applying for asylum? I'm afraid to return to my country because I fear being arrested, detained, tortured, and even killed by the authorities because of my political activities against the ruling party.
  • Were you ever physically harmed or threatened? How many times? I was physically harmed five times. I don't know exactly how many times I was threatened, but at least 10.

4. Details of Incidents

  • When did these five incidents occur? First time: February 3rd; detained for 8 days, beaten on Feb 4th, 5th, 9th; last time: March 15—all last year.
  • Were you physically harmed after March 15? No.
  • Describe in detail what happened the first time. Attending opposition party meeting, police burst in, shouting. Leader accused us of anti-government activities, we were handcuffed, I was beaten and separated for defending a colleague, taken to police station in different car.
  • Can you explain in detail about the ride from your friend's house to the police station? I was in pain, afraid I had a concussion. Handcuffed in back seat, officers in front, bumpy ride, didn't know destination, tried asking, got yelled at. Drive was over an hour, then arrived at unfamiliar police station.
  • Where was the jail cell? Ground floor in the back of police station.
  • Were you in the same cell all 8 days? Yes.
  • Please describe the jail cell. All by myself, very small—less than half the size of this office. Hard concrete floor, slept with rolled-up shirt as pillow, very hot, no windows, flickering light, bucket used as toilet, never emptied, many flies, dirty smell, one wall of metal bars at the door.
  • Were you taken out for interrogation? Yes, three times.
  • Describe the interrogation room. Bigger than cell. Police at metal desk, folder with papers, metal chair, two officers in corner, bright lights, no windows.
  • What happened during first interrogation? Asked to give names of opposition members, funding, methods. I replied I didn't know anything, said it was my first meeting; they called me liar and beat me.
  • How did they beat you? Pulled hair, slapped face, strapped arms to chair, beat me with rubber batons—on both arms, legs, stomach, sides.
  • How long did it last? Several minutes, felt like forever. More pain when I cried out.
  • What did they say while beating you? Called me a traitor, said they'd make me talk, threatened me.
  • Were you beaten during the other interrogations? Yes, beatings were similar but more painful due to bruises. Third time they poured cold water over my face, felt like drowning, could hardly breathe.

5. Life in Detention

  • How did you spend your time in detention? Time moved slowly; pain from beatings, covered in bruises, cell got more filthy, never allowed to shower or change clothes, slept on hard floor with rolled shirt as pillow, only fed once a day—bad food with bugs, one bottle of water to last all day, mostly just thinking and worrying alone.
  • Why were you finally released? Don't know, just taken out of cell and released without explanation.
  • Did they have you sign documents before release? No.
  • Did you receive any record of arrest/detention? No.
  • Did they say anything when you were released? Warned me not to oppose government again, threatened imprisonment and disappearance next time.
  • Did you seek medical attention? No, feared informants; used painkillers and ointment at home.

6. Activities After Detention

  • Did you continue political activities? Yes, but only via encrypted email due to fear.
  • Any other encounters with authorities? Yes, during March 15th protest.
  • Where did protest happen? Freedom Square, capital city—about 5,000 people.
  • What did you do at protest? Passed out pamphlets, spoke to protesters, led chants, protested peacefully for democracy and against abuses.
  • Did protest become violent? Yes, when authorities arrived—riot police attacked protesters, tear gas thrown, chaos, people beaten.
  • Were you harmed at protest? Yes, hit with batons by police, fell, kept being hit, called "rat" by riot police, helped out by other protesters.
  • Did you go home after? No, went to safe house; heard police searched for me at home.

7. Escape and Leaving Country

  • When did you leave your country? May 2nd, after hiding for a month and a half.
  • When did you apply for US visa? Second week of February, granted in the first week of March.
  • Why not leave earlier when visa granted? Wanted to attend protest, was not ready for the big decision to leave yet.
  • Any problems leaving? No, hired broker to bribe officials, got through safely.

8. Fear of Return

  • What do you fear would happen if you return? Arrest at airport, further imprisonment, interrogation, torture, disappearance ("disappeared"), possible death. Authorities harsh toward political opponents.
  • Could you live safely elsewhere in your country? Definitely not. Authorities share information across country; nowhere is safe, would have to live in hiding.

9. Interview Conclusion & Standard Questions

  • Did you understand all the questions? Yes.
  • Anything else you'd like to tell me? No, I've told you the most important details.
  • Have you ever lived/traveled in any country other than home or US? No.
  • Requested asylum/refugee status elsewhere? No.
  • Held official government position? No.
  • Served in the military, police, or law enforcement? No.
  • Member of group that engages in terrorist activities? No.
  • Have you ever been arrested/detained? Yes. Just the one time I've told you about today.
  • Committed crime not arrested for? No.
  • Intent to commit illegal acts in US? No.
